Cyber Notes Format





Section 1: Justification

This online presentation format is designed to be engaging and interactive, and uses the concept of chunking to break down essential course information into smaller "chunks" that can be more easily remembered and understood. Chunking also means that the sections are easy to reorganize, omit, or hide and can even be relocated to a different set of notes.

The notes also provide features like a pop-up index that allows students to locate and go directly to a specific subtopic when working on exercises or a project

Another feature is a "Try it" interactive feature for coding or design course notes. This allows students to apply/test their knowledge without opening an editor or another tab.

Section 9: Expanding sections

Rather than using a mouseover as seen earlier, use can click on a plus sign to expand a portion of the lecture. Here are some of the benefits of this type of notes:

  • Not Powerpoint!

    "Power corrupts. Powerpoint corrupts absolutely."
    Have you ever been an audience participant viewing an endless PowerPoint presentation...counting minutes and slides until the ordeal is finally over?
    Death by PowerPoint implies a really bad presentation. This might include text-heavy slides, confusing graphics, a presenter reading out 50 slides before a yawning audience or even a dysfunctional slide deck, marred by technical glitches and the inability of the presenter to cope with the pressure of presenting his/her slides.

  • More complete content

    One objection to Powerpoint is that only the main points are presented (often as bullet lists) but the presenter (hopefully) fills in the gaps verbally. But what if you missed class? What if you missed an important point while recording notes about a previous point?
    Instructors can use expanding sections to provide the missing content, and decide to reveal it or not. One can still embrace brevity while providing the essential details, whether they are revealed in the lecture or not.

  • Easily shared with students

    With the notes on the web, as long as they have access to a browser and Internet students can view them anywhere.
    You can accomplish the same thing with Powerpoint presentations, but students either have to have access to Powerpoint or the instructor has to have published the presentation to the web. But it's still Powerpoint!

  • Sidebars

    Sidebars started in print media as a short news story or graphic accompanying and presenting sidelights of a major story.
    An instructor can accomplish the same thing using an aside, like the one seen to the right, to provide background information or additional details about the topic.
    I often use them to provide information that may interest some students, but isn't essential information - and isn't on the exam!

  • Not static

    A presentation does not have to be static. The text inside any HTML element can be made editable by adding the contenteditable attribute.
    Students can use it to take notes, instructors can use it to work out problems - it's cool!
    One person wrote "It’s amazing that adding one attribute to an HTML element enables typing, selection, keyboard navigation, spell checking, drag and drop, pasting, and undo capabilities."
    Oh, this paragraph is editable if you want to give it a whirl.

Example of an aside

The aside element represents a section of a page that consists of content that is tangentially related to the content around the aside element, and which could be considered separate from that content.

Such sections are often represented as sidebars in printed typography.

Section 12: Summary

Presentations are essential in class, whether you teach by lecture, use a flipped classroom, or teach remotely.

  • Powerpoint has many advantages, but instructors who want to liven things up may prefer a different approach.

This online notes format is not the solution to every presentation, but my students have indicated that they greatly prefer this format over Powerpoint.

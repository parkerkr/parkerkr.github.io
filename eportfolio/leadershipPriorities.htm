Priorities as a Leader

There is a set of traits that I consider vital to a leader in any setting, but especially in academia. These traits help guide my approach to any leadership role.

  • Supportive: An academic leader must advocate for their college and faculty, even while supporting students, administrative superiors, and other stakeholders.
  • Fair: All faculty members should be treated equitably and, whenever possible, equally. No faculty member should ever feel marginalized. Openness, fairness, and integrity are required of all leaders.Leadership Philosophy
  • Considerate A leader should treat others with consideration. A leader should set a good example, listen and respond thoughtfully to people's concerns, rely on the wisdom and experience of others, and care about those with whom he or she works.
  • Receptive: A leader must be willing to listen to honest feedback. Every leader needs people who are not afraid to call attention to ill-advised decisions. A successful leader must be honest, fair, and open to new ideas, and must realize that he or she isn't always right.
  • Transparent: While a leader must take into account a multitude of political, financial, and environmental factors when making any decision, he or she should share facts and constraints with faculty whenever possible.
  • Collaborative: Academic leaders must embrace shared governance, and whenever possible involve faculty in the decision-making process. A leader should work toward establishing a consensus whenever possible, but avoid leading by consensus. A leader should not be afraid to rely on others for help and must be willing to delegate.
  • Agile: A leader must be forward-thinking, proactive, innovative, and willing to embrace change. This is especially critical to a leader in any technology-driven STEM discipline.
  • Transformative: A leader must lead faculty through change by identifying where changes need to be made and then moving the department in the corresponding direction.
  • Decisive: While it is critical to gather as many facts as possible before making decision, it is vital to be able to make a decision.
  • Stakeholder-oriented: Administrators are responsible for implementing policies for central administration, are the primary spokespersons for and advocates of their faculty, staff, and students, and serve as ambassadors to the external community, including businesses, recruiters, and alumni.

One of my former deans described me as “an energetic colleague who performs all components of his job with considerable skill and insight.” A faculty member once observed that “Kevin has a rare combination of administrative prowess, sagacity, and cheeriness. He is a great department chair who trusts his faculty and treats them as experts, colleagues, and friends and not as subordinates.” Another department member expressed appreciation for my leadership “because we're always moving forward, always moving toward a goal.”

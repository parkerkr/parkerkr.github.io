Research Interests

My research record shows a tightly focused, ongoing exploration of the skills and knowledge that information technology graduates need and how they can be best prepared to obtain it.

That research falls into three major streams. The following sections briefly describe each research stream, accompanied by a list of publications associated with each.

  • Stream 1: Improved Teaching of Core IT Courses

    This stream focuses on the integrated IS support of teaching.

    It includes programming language selection in light of industry trends and learning needs, as well as the development of teaching cases and projects, IT-enhanced teaching approaches including virtual teaching assistants and online support for courses, the applications of Web 2.0 and social media in education, teaching techniques and content selection for IT courses, and alternative instructional strategies in an IS/IT curriculum.

    • Category 1.1 – Programming Language Selection and Teaching Approaches

      The papers in this category propose criteria for the selection of programming languages for IS or IT curricula in light of industry trends and learning needs. The purpose of this sub-stream is not only to facilitate curriculum and course development, but also to ensure that students are given the opportunity to use the most appropriate tools and languages. The first paper, as well as the last three, discuss criteria for the selection of programming languages for introductory programming courses. The second paper explores pedagogy that promotes mastery of programming language syntax before attempting to developing programming solutions for assignments.

      • Parker K.R., Beard, D.V., & Davey, B. (2019). Programming Language Selection for University Courses. In A. Tatnall (Ed.), Encyclopedia of Education and Information Technologies Boston, MA: Springer, Cham.
      • Edwards, J. M., Fulton, E. K. Holmes, J. D., Valentin, J. L., Beard, D. V. & Parker, K. R. (2018). Separation of syntax and problem solving in Introductory Computer Programming. Proceedings of 48th Annual IEEE Frontiers in Education Conference (FIE 2018), San Jose, California: October 3-6.

    • Category 1.4 – Applications of Web 2.0 and Social Media in Education

      The papers in this category deal with the use of social media to enhance teaching. It is a more specific branch of the previous category. The first paper discusses using Wikis to teach students the value of interaction and collaboration, empowering education through openness and flexibility. The second paper attempts to involve several types of social media in teaching, including chat, Twitter, Facebook, and wikis. The last two papers focus exclusively on wikis, the first on wikis in general and the second on wiki use in education.

    • Category 1.5 – Enhanced IT Course Content

      The papers in this category seem diverse, but all relate to the design of curriculum or course content.

      The first and second papers identify a range of problems encountered in group projects and present several promising solutions.

      The third paper considers the value of requiring computer science majors to take a systems analysis and design course, even in lieu of a software engineering course, because many CS graduates enter the workforce designing business application software. This approach not only provides knowledge of software development techniques, but also exposure to concepts like business rules, varied stakeholders, and business requirements elicitation.

      The fourth paper presents an Information Technology Pedagogical Knowledge framework intended to facilitate the design and development of IT-related curriculum.

      The fifth and seventh papers discuss the content of information technology hardware and systems software courses.

      The sixth paper presents an instructional method for validating a relational database design.

  • Stream 2: Impact of Developments in Information Systems on Curriculum

    This stream includes software selection for courses in light of industry trends and learning needs, emerging approaches in software development, information assurance in systems analysis and design, and the application of IT to healthcare.

    The focus of this research is to ensure that both programming languages and software tools prepare IS/IT graduates for the real world.

    • Category 2.1 – Teaching IT: Software Selection and Emerging Programming Teaching Approaches

      The papers in this category focus on software tool selection for IT courses as well as emerging approaches for software develoment, like frameworks and architectures. The first paper examines software design techniques, which focus on reusable software architectural structures such as frameworks and design patterns used to facilitate software development. The second paper explores the use of frameworks in teaching software development, and how to make the most appropriate selection. The third paper covers the evaluation and selection of software tools for use in an IS or IT curriculum.

      • Holmes, J.D., Parker, K.R., Davey, B., & Chao, J.T. (2019). Teaching Software Design Techniques in University Courses. In A. Tatnall (Ed.), Encyclopedia of Education and Information Technologies Boston, MA: Springer, Cham.

  • Stream 3: Emerging Systems Development Issues

    This stream includes computing education trends, or using historical trends to anticipate what is coming next.

    It also includes research into emerging trends in development, like the Semantic Web.

    • Category 3.4 – Web Accessibility Needs

      This sub-stream concentrates on web accessibility issues, such as the reading level of the content of a web site content. The first paper in this series considers the difficulties that an aging population might encounter with a government web site. Many design courses address obvious issues like the Web Accessibility Initiative – Accessible Rich Internet Applications guidelines. However, while developers can accommodate disabled users by developing screen-reader accessible sites, and can even plan to accommodate users with color blindness, they must also be taught not to overlook less obvious barriers, like readability.
